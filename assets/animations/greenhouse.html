THE SCIENCE

The Physics of Warming

The greenhouse effect is driven by the fundamental laws of thermodynamics. The Sun (~5,500°C) emits shortwave radiation (visible light). Earth's atmosphere is largely transparent to these wavelengths.

When sunlight strikes Earth, the planet absorbs it and warms up. Because Earth is much cooler, it radiates energy back as longwave infrared radiation. Certain atmospheric gases trap this specific wavelength — that's the greenhouse effect.

Major Greenhouse Gases

Carbon Dioxide (CO₂)

The primary driver of modern climate change. Atmospheric levels have surged from 280 ppm (pre-industrial) to over 425 ppm today — a 50% increase. CO₂ persists in the atmosphere for 300–1,000 years.

Methane (CH₄)

Over 80× more potent than CO₂ over 20 years. Released by livestock, rice paddies, landfills, and natural gas leaks. Concentrations have more than doubled since pre-industrial times.

Nitrous Oxide (N₂O) & Water Vapor

N₂O is linked to synthetic fertilizers and is ~300× more potent than CO₂. Water vapor is the most abundant greenhouse gas, creating a feedback loop — warmer air holds more moisture, amplifying warming.

Natural vs. Enhanced

The greenhouse effect is essential for life. Without it, Earth's average temperature would be a freezing -18°C instead of 15°C.

The crisis is the enhanced effect. By burning fossil fuels and clearing forests, humanity has thickened the atmospheric "blanket," trapping heat that would normally escape into space.

Global Impacts

• Ocean Acidification: Oceans absorb ~30% of excess CO₂, lowering pH by 26% since 1850, threatening coral reefs and shellfish.

• Sea Level Rise: Ice sheet melt and thermal expansion have raised seas ~20 cm since 1900. Projections: 0.3–1.0m more by 2100.

• Extreme Weather: Extra heat energy fuels stronger hurricanes, prolonged droughts, and severe flooding with increasing frequency.
