OCEAN ACIDIFICATION

The Ocean Sink

The ocean absorbs about 30% of all CO₂ released by human activities. While this slows atmospheric warming, it fundamentally changes seawater chemistry.

The Chemical Reaction

CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃ → H⁺ + HCO₃⁻

CO₂ reacts with water to form Carbonic Acid, which splits into Bicarbonate and free Hydrogen ions (H⁺).

The flood of H⁺ ions lowers ocean pH, making it more acidic.

The pH Scale

Since the industrial revolution, surface ocean pH dropped from 8.2 to 8.1 — a 30% increase in acidity (logarithmic scale).

Impact on Marine Life

• Stolen Building Blocks: H⁺ ions bond with carbonate that corals, oysters, and shelled plankton need to build shells.

• Coral Bleaching: Stressed corals expel symbiotic algae, turning bone white. Over 50% of the Great Barrier Reef has bleached since 2016.

• Food Web Collapse: Tiny shelled organisms at the base of the food chain dissolve in acidic water, impacting fish, whales, and billions of humans.

💡 AI Insight

At current emission rates, ocean pH could drop to 7.8 by 2100 — a 150% acidity increase from pre-industrial levels. This would make 90% of coral reefs unviable and dissolve the shells of pteropods, collapsing marine food chains that feed 3 billion people.

Based on IPCC AR6, NOAA Ocean Acidification Program.
